On Wed, Dec 30, 2009 at 5:37 PM, Rory Blyth <lists at rory.me> wrote:
> It would be fantastic if we could develop cross-platform apps like
> that, but there are so many things that get in the way that it's best
> left as a good idea (for now).
>
> To get the nice resolution-independent vector-based UIs, you couldn't
> work with CocoaTouch - you'd have to have your own rendering engine.
> That'd be fine, except that, for native apps, Apple has strict
> guidelines about how your app shoud look/feel/work. Even small
> deviations - *when using their own frameworks* - can get your app
> rejected. For the first app I worked on, I had to resubmit after
> redoing the main UI so it didn't use a grid of buttons for kicking off
> various part of the app. Yes: buttons. They were right, actually - I
> should've been using a table, and rather than buttons, I should have
> had cells.
>
> It feels at firs like they're meddling, but the benefits far outweigh
> the limitations. By requiring devs to follow the guidelines, Apple
> practically did away with the need to give your users manuals. When
> you think about it, you've probably never, or rarely, ever had to
> consult a manual or help screen for an iPhone app. That's partly due
> to the consistency, and that consistency is largely due to the
> similarity of one UI to the next. There are some rules and there are
> some conventions (like expecting that tapping the status bar will
> scroll you to the top of a screen that vertically extends beyond the
> device's resolution).
>
> You'd have to give up much of what's already there for you. For
> example, the keyboard - your rendering engine would need a lot of code
> for managing the keyboard (you don't control the iPhone's keyboard -
> at best, you influence it). That'd lead to temptation to make your
> own. But then you'd have to create something that would look just like
> Apple's, only you wouldn't have the "free" spellchecker, and there
> would undoubtedly be differences, however subtle, between it and the
> real keyboard.
>
> iPhone users can be really sensitive to anything non-standard.
> Something I do from time to time to get a feel for what'll fly and
> what won't is to wander through the AppStore in no particular order,
> randomly tapping on apps to read the reviews.
>
> Something I've found, for example, is that many users complain about
> web apps. I'm talking about the apps in the store that are little more
> that a launcher for a UIWebView that loads up some remote mobile site
> (albeit iPhone-optimized). Although you can do quite a bit with iPhone
> web apps, people can tell the difference. I've seen apps slammed for
> being non-native (the Bank of America app comes to mind - I use it,
> and I cringe every time).
>
> When things aren't *just right*, then, a surprisingly large number of
> users will notice. It's weird. Users don't typically pick up on things
> like that. There are devs who can do quite a lot with iPhone web apps,
> but they'll never *feel* the way an iPhone app is "supposed" to.
>
> Point being, as a dev, I want what you're describing - have wanted it
> for years (mainly because of Windows Mobile - MS just hands off WM to
> third-parties who then create devices with all sorts of features, like
> VGA screens, that other WM devices lack - the word "argh" comes to
> mind).
>
> What you're left with, realistically, is a set of options that favor
> the dev but not the consumer:
>
> - Inconsistent user experience
>
> - To be cross-platform, apps would have to limit themselves to the
> most basic subset of controls common across the platforms. There are
> very iPhone-specific controls and frameworks (like nav and tab bar
> controllers, MapKit, etc.) that either wouldn't make sense on the
> other platforms or would be difficult to implement (to the point that
> you might as well write your app for each platform you want to target).
>
> - You'd be subject to divergence in platforms. Like, Android might
> move in a completely different direction that could complicate this
> abstraction framework. Android is already basically the second coming
> of Windows Mobile - I don't know what's going to happen with it, but
> Google's approach has been very similar to Microsoft's, and you can
> count on trouble. With the Google/Microsoft method, "cross-platform"
> has a different meaning than it does in this coversation. With WM,
> your app is "cross-platform" if you can get it running properly on the
> majority of devices running *the same* OS. That's one reason iPhone
> development is so attractive: Apple boxes us in, but that also frees
> us from having to worry about targeting this or that model of iPhone
> (there are exceptions to this, but you can still take advantage of
> most of what the iPhone has to offer regardless of what generation
> iPhone you're targeting). I realize that the Apple tablet (which is
> supposed to be announced in January (though not sold till mid-year))
> changes this since it's running iPhone OS, and it will clearly have
> different capabilities from the iPhone. My guess is that very few apps
> will be written to support both platforms. It'll be one or the other.
>
> - Unless this framework were freakishly well implemented, you'd likely
> end up with apps that Apple would never approve (because they wouldn't
> conform to Apple's guidelines).
>
> - The effort required to code all this up is probably not ultimately
> worth it. Done well, it's a good idea, but there are *so* many
> obstacles.
>
> I don't mean to be a bummer here, but I've pondered the same thing.
>
> There are people out there now who're doing cross-platform tools, but
> many of the issues I mentioned here are present in them.
>
> PhoneGap is a tool that provides a way of taking advantage of native
> device features from mobile web apps. You write your app as a mobile
> web app, access native features (like GPS and accelerometers) through
> a JavaScript API that handles calling the native bits for you. You
> then have the tool spit out project files for the platform of your
> choice. What you end up with is, in the case of the iPhone, an Xcode
> project that creates a web view for you and hosts your mobile app. You
> build your app and then submit it to the store. I'm not a fan, as I
> bought my iPhone because I want to take advantage of *all* it can do,
> and that goes well beyond the functionality PhoneGap gives you access
> to. It has its place, but... yeah. I just spent $500 to upgrade to a
> 3G S, and I want apps that are going to justify that cost.
>
> Another tool is Titanium Appelerator. I think that's what it's called
> - tapping this on my phone and don't really want to check.
>
> If I recall, that one produces native apps for the iPhone and Android.
> Don't remember about others. You code using HTML/JavaScript/etc., and
> then, if I understood the manual, creates actual native apps. The
> drawback is that, although you'll get native widgets, you're still
> bound to the most basic subset of functionalty shared across the
> platforms. I see this as a step up from PhoneGap, but... still not
> good enough.
>
> What I love about MonoTouch is that it does one thing, and it does
> that thing extremely well: it makes iPhone apps easier to develop, and
> I can use nearly every feature the iPhone has. And if there's a native
> API I want access to, but that isn't supported by MT out of the box, I
> can easily create a binding for it. Or, if I want to use some old ObjC
> code of my own, I can use it as a library from my MT project.
>
> Like I said, I'm not trying to be a bummer - just rambling on with my
> own thought about the subject.
>
> On the desktop, you can *almost* get away with cross-platform apps,
> but it's just never worked out well. Mobile devices are a much more
> difficult a target for this because of how much one platform (and even
> different devices running the same OS) differs from the next.
>
> Not saying it wouldn't be nice :)
>
> - Rory
