>> "If you have the budget to pay for senior developers, this is not a problem;
>>  If you have the budget to pay for double the programmers, this is not a problem;  If you have the budget to pay for the extra time it will take to

>> But not everyone can afford that, and that is why language design continues to evolve and that is why languages like C# and Java continue to improve 
>> year after year, because they help reduce defect rates, and increase programmer productivity."


Thank you Miguel.

This is exactly my frustration and concern regarding the new policy... 

My firm isn't going to invest significant $$$ in training staff to learn these tools. We have a TON of time and resources invested in .NET based libraries that we (in theory) could leverage on the iPhone using monotouch. We have a staff of 25 developers who are well versed in C#/.NET who have no practical experience with objective-C, CocoaTouch or XCode. 

The new policy (in effect) would require my firm to completely rewrite/port these libraries to Objective-C, which over time would be a maintenance nightmare. It would also require us to cross-train developers in Objective-C, which has no practical benefit outside of iPhone development.

Now, one exclusion to this is that applications intended for "enterprise" or "in house distribution" are NOT subject to this provision. The provision only applies to applications being hosted on the app store. This *does* soften the blow for us, as most of the applications in development are intended for internal-use only.... but it does raise the ugly issue that we will still need forked environments for internal vs. public apps.


Bottom line: This new policy is garbage. I am a *huge* apple proponent, but this xenophobic attitude has gone too far.
