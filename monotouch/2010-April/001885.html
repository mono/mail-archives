Thanks for this, the context of the replies is very telling.

There is only 2 reasons I can think of why they would issue the following reply to your request on using MonoTouch.
"Our agreement is aimed at developers who want to distribute their apps through the App Store "

1) MonoTouch and all other related technology is banned. But rather than reply 'Yes our new monopolist strategy effectively renders your effort and investments with MonoTouch futile' they choose to sugar coat it and make it sound like it will only be limited via the 'App store channel' but we still allow you the freedom to explore every other opportunity.

2) It is not a direct answer so they haven't fully made their intentions clear and that they are likely processing the negative-sentiment and media backlash before making there official stance.

Based on the wording in that reply, my conclusion is that MonoTouch is currently prohibited but they may be leaving themselves room to reverse their decision if it turns out to be a catastrophic one.

On Sat, Apr 10, 2010 at 12:18 AM, <Steve.Mentzer at lw.com<mailto:Steve.Mentzer at lw.com>> wrote:
I am not at liberty to disclose the exact wording of my email, as it contains information sensitive to my firm.

Paraphrasing, this is what I said...

"We are developing applications for the iPhone using MonoTouch. We are using MonoTouch because we have a large pool of talent and work product written in C#.  Your new policy appears to prohibit the use of MonoTouch because it is not an officially sanctioned language. This would require us to throw away and rewrite a large portion of this work product, which is likely a deal breaker for my firm. Please re-think this policy."

His reply:

"Our agreement is aimed at developers who want to distribute their apps through the App Store "

My reply:

"We intend to develop both in-house *and* app store applications"

His reply:

"We would not accept them into the App Store if they violate our terms. Sorry. "

>From the new agreement:

"Applications must be originally written in Objective-C, C, C++, or JavaScript as executed by the iPhone OS WebKit engine"

That is pretty clear-cut. If you want to have your app included in the app store, it must be written in Objective-C, C or C++.....   and any javascript can ONLY be executed by the iPhone OS or the webkit engine (UIWebView).

Personally, I think apple's stance on this is ridiculous. I understand them wanting to choke off the compiled-flash option, but the collateral damage here is amazing.
