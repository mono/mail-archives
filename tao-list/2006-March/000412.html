distributions?

> forward.
>
> A few points:
>
> - despite its origins as a games-oriented framework of native bindings,
> in practice, Tao is probably better described as a loose confederation
> of native bindings conforming to what I like to call the "Tao
> philosophy" (definition below).  Games-relatedness is one aspect, but
> many of the native libraries have other uses too, and many non-game
> applications can be created in .NET/Mono using Tao.  I'm not sure
> finding a maintainer of "the Tao whole" is necessarily the best approach
> - unifying the maintainers of the various pieces of Tao around a central
> core of guidelines might work just as well, and be easier to sustain.
>
> - the various native libraries that Tao rests upon are more or less
> independent of each other, move at different paces, have different
> issues, appeal to different audiences and meet different needs.  In the
> non-windows world, at least, the native libraries are often/typically
> part of a web of dependencies that extends to other parts of the
> (operating) system and perhaps the Tao bindings should be more in tune
> with that reality.  Think of the various linux distributions and how
> most shared libraries are installed at the system level rather than
> locally.  They are generally involved in dependency relationships to
> other libraries and applications in the overall context of the
> distribution.  The dependency relationship between a particular Tao
> binding, its native library, and possible .NET-based applications can
> be/are closer than the relationship between the various Tao bindings.
> The same holds true to a lesser degree for Windows, though the ecosystem
> is different, since most of the native libs are not part of the 'core'
> windows platform.
>
> - the various Tao bindings are currently maintained by different
> individuals rather than a single person and have different audiences.
>
> So rather than having a single person responsible for Tao, maybe a
> better approach is to have everybody work together to define a set of
> guidelines and unifying principles that would qualify a binding to be
> part of the "Tao family".  Individual maintainers/teams would be
> responsible for the different bindings and would maintain them and
> produce releases according to the dictates of their respective
> communities and underlying native libraries.  This makes more sense to
> me than creating an artificial bottleneck in the form of a single
> project maintainer who has to manage and decide on when to do monolithic
> Tao releases.  It also makes it easier to deal with personnel issues -
> when life circumstances change, it's easier to find new maintainers of a
> particular binding than maintainers of Tao as a whole.  Certain
> activities (adding .NET 2.0 support, for example) would require the
> cooperation of the various Tao maintainers, so Tao as a unifying concept
> still makes sense.
>
> What is the general opinion regarding this kind of approach as opposed
> to the "Tao as a monolithic project" approach?
>
> * The Tao Philosophy:  A good Tao binding is a low-level, high quality,
> low level binding to a particular native library which sticks to the
> conventions and architecture of the library as closely as possible even
> when these deviate from conventional .NET ideoms.  A Tao binding is a
> thin veneer over the native library that should feel comfortable to
> someone familiar with that library, and artifacts associated with that
> library (documentation, coding conventions, etc.) should be immediately
> useful.  To provide an interface more comfortable to a developer
> expecting .NET ideoms, a 'sister' framework can be created on top of the
> Tao binding which presents a more comfortable interface conforming to
> .NET ideoms to a user.  Examples of this are Tao.SDL/SDL.NET and
> Tao.Ode/OdeDotNet.  This model seems comfortable to people I've
> discussed it with.
