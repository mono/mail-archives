> Xamarin had to introduce hacks like "System.nint" and "System.nuint" to
> solve the problem in the world of Apple interop.
>
>
>
> If we support operators for IntPtr and UIntPtr, then you won’t get
> operators for nint and nuint.  It sounds like having a general operator
> story might be a better idea that trying to continually bless specific
> types in the compiler.
>

I would get them for free.   Let me explain why.

In C# "int" is a shorthand for System.Int32 and "double" to System.Double
and so on.

We would get the operator support in System.IntPtr, and if we get the nice
alias, we would just get them for free. [1]


> > It is only tiny, because we have made it so cumbersome to use.
>
> > Xamarin had to introduce hacks like "System.nint" and "System.nuint" to
> solve the problem in the world of Apple interop.
>
>
>
> Can you give more data on this.  I’m familiar with coding for iOS, but I’d
> like to know the cases where:
>
>    1. Someone really needed to be using these types
>    2. A solution that brought in operators through another library would
>    not be sufficient.
>
> They are used pretty much everywhere on iOS today, you can not escape it.
  They are the NSInteger and CGFloat data types that you see everywhere in
Objective-C and Int/CGFloat on Swift.   Every one of those parameters on
UITableView is now an NSInteger.

Nowadays on iOS/macOS, it is very rare to find regular int32_t or floats.

Apple started a transition at some point where every API that had an "int"
switched to be an NSInteger and floats became CGFloats.   At the time, most
people -me included- thought that NSInteger was just Apple being cute.
 And then the 64-bit iPhone came out, and BAM!   There it was, Apple had
pulled the switch.
