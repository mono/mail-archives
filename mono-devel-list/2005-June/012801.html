The cell processor is supposed to be quite flexible (and it is *very* fast)
so I think even the normal computing power of PS3 will be impressive (same
goes for PowerPC CPU used in Xbox 360 and Nintendo revolution). Enabling an
OS to run on PS3 (IMHO) makes a lot of sense.
 
True, the consoles are heavily optimised for graphics, but that's really
where the GPU's come in, and the absolutely staggering (teraflops!) floating
point numbers. To put it into perspective, Blue Gene/L is currently the
world's fastest supercomputer
(http://news.bbc.co.uk/1/hi/technology/4379261.stm) capable of 135.5
teraflops by using 32,768 processors. At 1.8 teraflops, the PS3 floating
point performance is 1/75 that of Blue Gene/L's, or equivalent to a Blue
Gene with 435 processors (for $400!). One cannot fail to be impressed. Many
modern day computing tasks (3D image rendering, video editing, and many
forms of simulation and number crunching) can be done on GPU's, using their
highly parallel processing engines. E.g. there have been (for years now)
experimental ray-tracing and global-illumination implementations which run
on nVidia's GPU's. So in addition to the (very capable) cell processor, many
tasks can be offloaded to the GPU, and clustering (as you mentioned) may be
a very real possibility.
 
I hope you are right about Xbox's (semi) native CLI support. Right now I
think that Mono has evolved to a point where what is good for .NET is good
for Mono. By marketing .NET, MS is also making Mono more attractive. A
unified platform (.NET/Mono) across OS's is good news for everyone. And if
MS is gaining an advantage via native CLI support then others are bound to
reap the same rewards since the CPU's of all three (new) consoles are basic
IBM (PowerPC) designs. Well, who knows, at the moment it's mostly
speculation, but the future for Mono is looking bright.
 
Pete.
